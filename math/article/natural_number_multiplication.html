Multiplication of Natural Numbers

Informal description

Prerequisites

  • Natural Numbers

Suppose we have some groups of the same size (e.g. boxes containing the same amount of fruit), then the total number of objects can be determined by the the size of the groups and the number of groups. If the size of the groups is \(a\) and the number of groups is \(b\), the total number of objects is defined as the product of \(a\) and \(b\), denoted as \(a \times b\). Taking the product of two numbers is known as multiplication.

Multiplication can be written as repeated addition—the product of \(a\) and \(b\) is equal to the sum of \(b\) \(a\)s.

To count the number of objects arranged in a rectangle with width \(w\) and height \(h\), one could group the columns to arrive at \(w\) groups with \(h\) objects each, or group the rows to arrive at \(h\) groups with \(w\) objects each. These two approaches should arrive at the same answer, that is,

\[ a \times b = b \times a \]

Because the product of multiple numbers does not depend on the the order of evaluation, the product is often written without parentheses; for example, \((a \times b) \times c\) can be written as \(a \times b \times c\).
